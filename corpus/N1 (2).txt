Utility Functions
We use utility functions to summarize a consumer’s preferences. If a consumer
prefers one choice to another, we say that it yields her higher utility.
More formally, we are interested in the consumer’s choices between con-
sumption bundles. A consumption bundle is just a list of quantities of goods
the consumer might choose: for example, the set of goods could be (Coffee,
Tea), and the consumer might choose two cups of coffee and one cup of tea.
We would then write her choice as (2,1). If the consumer prefers, say, two cups
of coffee plus one cup of tea to three cups of coffee and no tea, we would say
that her utility function is higher at (2,1) than at (3,0). If we denote the utility
function by u, this is the same as saying u(2, 1) > u(3, 0). In general, if x is
one possible choice and x′ is another, and the consumer prefers x to x′, then we
expect her utility function to satisfy u(x) > u(x′).

Intuition
The key condition that the solution to the utility maximisation problem, denoted
x∗, should satisfy is
u′

for any pair of goods i and j.
There are several equivalent ways of saying
this in words. Most directly, the ratio of marginal utilities between any two
goods should equal the ratio of prices. More intuitively, the consumer should be
indifferent between buying a little more of good i and a little more of good j, for
any two goods i and j. If she were not, she could slightly reduce her consumption
of one good, raise her consumption of the other, and end up better off.
To make this argument concrete, let’s suppose there are two goods with
prices £1 for good 1 and £2 for good 2. Suppose the consumer considers an
affordable level of consumption (x1, x2) at which the marginal utility of good
1 is 5 and the marginal utility of good 2 is also 5.
If the consumer lowers
her consumption of good 2 by 1 unit her utility will fall by 5, and she will
save £2.
With this extra money, she can buy 2 units of good 1 and raise
her utility by 10. She has thus found a new bundle which yields 10 - 5 = 5
more units of utility and is also affordable; so the original bundle cannot be a
solution to her utility maximisation problem. The only time when such utility-
increasing rearrangement is not possible is when the marginal utility of each
good is proportional to its price.
Using this condition, plus the budget constraint, you can solve for demand
for each good xi as a function of prices and income. In general we can write
demand as
xi = Di(pi, p−i, y).
In words we would read this as: demand for good i is a function of the price of
good i, the price of all other goods, and income.
